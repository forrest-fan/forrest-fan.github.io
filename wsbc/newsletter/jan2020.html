How Technology is Driving the Future of Fan Engagement

Kenny Zhong - January 2020

The world has seen vast technological advancements over the past decade change the way we live our lives, and this is no different when it comes to sports. This continued evolution will be put on full display during the 2020 Tokyo Summer Olympics, as the year’s marquee sporting event looks to change the game.

Set in a country that is no stranger to pushing the boundaries of innovation, the 32nd edition of the Summer Games will be poised to deliver a fresh product as they try to appease a younger audience.

Leveraging the power of 5G, Olympic Broadcasting Services (OBS) has partnered with Intel and Alibaba in the creation of an AI tracking tool to be used in the collection of athletes’ real-time biomechanical data. We were able to catch glimpses of similar innovations at 2019’s Next Gen ATP Finals which allowed athletes to use wearable technologies to help players and coaches quantify performance. However, this time around broadcasters will be able to provide viewers with an in-depth look into what allows Olympians to compete at their level.

Virtual Reality (VR) has taken a little bit longer to reach the masses than previously expected, but it hasn’t been for a lack of trying. OBS and Intel will look to build upon VR’s introduction at the 2018 PyeongChang Winter Olympics to include immersive experiences for a larger number of events including the opening and closing ceremonies. Implementations of these technologies have been seen stateside as well; the Boston Red Sox have had a “VR Home Run Challenge” experience installed within the green confines of Fenway Park.

As with any large sporting event, long wait times are the bane of every enjoyable fan experience. At Safeco Field, home of the Seattle Mariners baseball club, biometric screening has allowed for faster entry into the stadium and a frictionless experience at concessions. The Tokyo Organizing Committee (TOCOG) has also taken strides to improve this key factor as TOCOG will look to use a “digital twin” (the virtual replication of a physical space) to help event planners simulate expected line queues and plan evacuation protocols without disrupting the event. In combination with facial recognition, these technologies will help to streamline fan movement across the various stadiums and make for a more secure environment.
