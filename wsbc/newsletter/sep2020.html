The Comeback Story

Kenny Zhong - September 2020

As the coronavirus continues to spread across the world, making its impact felt within educational, commercial, and healthcare institutions, the effect on the sporting world has been no different. With the postponement of marquee events like the Tokyo 2020 Olympics and of the regular seasons of major sporting leagues stateside, the pandemic has created a logistical nightmare for organizers in addition to the enormous financial ramifications. However across the pacific in New Zealand, rugby is being played with raucous fans in the stands, and to the east in Europe, Alphonso Davies continued his outstanding play en route to a Champions League title. So why exactly has it taken so long for professional sports to return in North America?

The obvious answer of course is how each country has been handling the virus. In Germany, Italy, and Spain where the disease was wreaking havoc only a few short months ago, governments have been able to effectively curb infections rates allowing for safer restarts of their respective soccer leagues. This is far from the current situation in the United States where the number of daily cases has never been higher. Leagues have needed to create exhaustive manuals outlining protocols as they prepare to return players in isolated environments. The NHL, NBA, and MLS saw and continue to see great success with this model despite several marquee players withdrawing from competition after doubting the safety of theses “bubbles”, wanting to maintain the health and well-being of themselves and their families.

Also holding up the comeback of professional sports has been the lengthy labour negotiations between player’s unions and the leagues. Lost ticket sales from no fans in the stands and reduced TV revenue from cancelled games have put leagues in a financial bind; forced into the reality of lowering salary caps for future seasons and demanding players and staff alike to take pay cuts. Perhaps no league better represents the ugly side of sport than the MLB. After a standoff between owners and players on how revenues should be shared in an abbreviated season, the two sides finally came to a deal that they had previously agreed upon three months prior. Suffice to say the optics aren’t great when multi-billionaires and multi-millionaires fight over a couple percent.

Even though it brought yet another crushing end to a Toronto Maple Leafs season, the safe return of sports amidst a year unlike any other was a great stepping stone towards our journey to a new normal.
